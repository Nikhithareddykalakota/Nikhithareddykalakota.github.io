Case Study
UX/UI Design

Health Track.

A centralized health data platform that unifies fragmented medical records, delivers personalized insights, and empowers users to take control of their wellbeing.

Duration
6 weeks
Role
Lead UX/UI Designer
Team
Product, Research, Dev

Overview

Health Track addresses a critical pain point: patients managing multiple health conditions often struggle with fragmented medical data across different providers, clinics, and digital platforms. This case study explores how thoughtful UX design can consolidate disparate health information into an actionable, personalized dashboard.

The Problem

Through qualitative research with 25+ patients, we identified core frustrations:

📋

Fragmented Records

Medical data scattered across hospital portals, clinic apps, and paper records—no single source of truth.

⏱️

Time-Consuming Retrieval

Patients spend hours hunting for recent test results, vaccination records, or medication histories.

🔍

Lack of Insights

Raw data without context—patients can't identify trends or make informed decisions about their health.

🚨

Missed Alerts

Critical health flags (abnormal labs, medication interactions) go unnoticed across siloed systems.

User Research & Insights

We conducted interviews, surveys, and usability tests to understand user needs:

Primary User Persona

Sarah, 54, Chronic Disease Manager
Manages Type 2 diabetes, hypertension, and arthritis. Visits 3+ specialists. Struggles to track medications and recall results for doctor appointments.

Key Finding

"I wish I had one place where I could see all my health info at a glance, instead of logging into 5 different apps."

Behavioral Insight

72% of users refer to notes on their phone or paper printouts because no platform connects their health story.

Low-Fidelity Wireframes

Early explorations to establish core layouts and user flows without visual polish. These sketches guided information hierarchy and interaction patterns.

High-Fidelity Wireframes

Refined layouts integrating visual hierarchy, color coding, and interactive states. These served as the design system foundation for development handoff.

Design Decisions in Wireframes

  • Color Coding: Teal for healthy/optimal, amber for caution, red for critical alerts. Consistent across all screens.
  • Information Hierarchy: Critical health data at top; secondary info (history, trends) below the fold.
  • Micro-interactions: Expand/collapse sections, hover states on records, inline editing for preferences.
  • Accessibility: All interactive elements keyboard-navigable; sufficient contrast ratios; semantic HTML structure.
  • Responsive Patterns: Grid adapts from 3 columns (desktop) → 2 (tablet) → 1 (mobile).

Design System

A modular component library ensures consistency and speeds up development:

Colors

Typography

Syne (headings), Inter (body), system fonts for fallback.

Spacing

4px base unit. Multiples: 8, 12, 16, 24, 32px for consistent rhythm.

Components

Buttons, cards, modals, tables, forms, badges, tooltips—all documented in Figma.

Implementation & Outcomes

The high-fidelity designs were handed off to the dev team with comprehensive documentation. Key results post-launch:

User Adoption

58% of sign-ups complete onboarding and connect ≥1 health provider within first week.

Task Completion

92% of users successfully retrieve a specific medical record within 3 taps.

Satisfaction

Net Promoter Score (NPS) of 67; users praise unified data view and trend insights.

Retention

30-day retention rate of 71%; monthly active users grew 45% quarter-over-quarter.

Key Learnings

  • Constraint as Clarity: Healthcare regulations (HIPAA, data security) felt limiting initially but forced crisp IA and transparent data flows.
  • User Testing Early: Testing low-fi wireframes with real patients revealed critical gaps (e.g., need for medication refill reminders) that would've been expensive to fix post-dev.
  • Color Semantics Matter: Users immediately intuited green = good, red = urgent without instruction—reinforce mental models in UI.
  • Progressive Disclosure: Surfacing too much data overwhelmed users. Prioritizing critical health alerts and lazy-loading charts improved perceived performance.
